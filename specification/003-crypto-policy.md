# Cryptographic and protocol-security policy

**Purpose.** This document defines Heimdall's cryptographic and protocol-security policy for the encrypted DNS transports. It collects the decisions that constrain TLS, QUIC, HTTP/2, and HTTP/3 behaviour across all roles, independently of which roles or transports are active in a given deployment. It does not redefine which transports Heimdall supports or when their listeners are instantiated; those questions are settled in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md).

**Status.** Stable.

**Requirement category.** `SEC`.

For the project-wide principles that frame these requirements (security non-negotiable, performance as the primary guide, "Assume Nothing"), see [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md). For specification-wide conventions, see [`README.md`](README.md). For the role model, see [`001-server-roles.md`](001-server-roles.md). For the transport set and the (role, transport) matrix to which this policy applies, see [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md).

## 1. Scope

This document governs the cryptographic and protocol-security behaviour of the encrypted DNS transports defined in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md):

- DNS-over-TLS (DoT) on port 853.
- DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) over HTTP/2.
- DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) over HTTP/3.
- DNS-over-QUIC (DoQ) on port 853.

The policy applies to incoming traffic on every (role, transport) combination permitted by `NET-011` in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md). Requirements that target a specific transport state so explicitly; requirements that target "all TLS-protected transports" apply uniformly to DoT and to DoH over HTTP/2.

The requirements of this document that fix protocol-version policy and early-data policy — specifically the TLS 1.3-only policy in section 2, the TLS 1.3 early-data prohibition in section 3.1, the QUIC version policy in section 6, and the QUIC 0-RTT prohibition in section 3.2 — additionally govern the outbound client-side behaviour of Heimdall wherever another specification document explicitly cross-references them. The forwarder's outbound transport obligations in `NET-015`, `NET-016`, and `NET-017` in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md) and the recursive resolver's outbound transport obligations in `NET-022` and `NET-023` in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md) are such cross-references. The remaining sections of this document (TLS session resumption, TLS and QUIC mTLS on the server, QUIC amplification mitigation and address validation, HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 hardening) describe server-side obligations and do not, on their own, govern outbound client behaviour.

## 2. TLS version policy

### 2.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-001.** Heimdall MUST accept only TLS 1.3 handshakes on every TLS-protected transport. This applies to DoT on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/2.
- **SEC-002.** Heimdall MUST reject every handshake that negotiates, or attempts to negotiate, any TLS version earlier than TLS 1.3, including TLS 1.2, TLS 1.1, TLS 1.0, and all SSL versions.
- **SEC-003.** The TLS 1.2 code path in the underlying TLS stack MUST be disabled at build or configuration time so that TLS 1.2 handshake processing is not reachable at runtime on any TLS-protected transport.
- **SEC-004.** For DNS-over-QUIC and for DNS-over-HTTPS over HTTP/3, the TLS layer MUST be the TLS 1.3 instance integrated into QUIC as specified by [RFC 9001](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9001). Requirements `SEC-001`, `SEC-002`, and `SEC-003` are consistent with this path; QUIC-specific policy beyond the TLS version is governed by sections 6 and 7 and by the open questions in section 9.

## 3. Early data (0-RTT) policy

### 3.1 TLS-over-TCP transports (DoT and DoH over HTTP/2)

- **SEC-005.** Heimdall MUST NOT accept TLS 1.3 early data (0-RTT) on any TLS-protected transport. This applies to DoT on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/2.
- **SEC-006.** Heimdall MUST reject the `early_data` extension when it is offered by the client on the transports covered by `SEC-005`, and any early-data bytes received before the completion of the TLS handshake MUST be discarded without being processed as DNS or HTTP input.
- **SEC-007.** No configuration knob that enables TLS 1.3 early data on the transports covered by `SEC-005` MAY be exposed at this stage.

### 3.2 QUIC-based transports (DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3)

- **SEC-022.** Heimdall MUST NOT accept QUIC 0-RTT application data on any QUIC-based transport. This applies to DoQ on port 853 ([RFC 9250](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9250)) and to DoH over HTTP/3 ([RFC 9114](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9114)).
- **SEC-023.** Heimdall MUST reject or discard any 0-RTT packets received from clients on the transports covered by `SEC-022`, and MUST require clients to complete the 1-RTT QUIC handshake before any application data is accepted. No byte carried in a 0-RTT packet MAY be processed as DNS or HTTP input.
- **SEC-024.** No configuration knob that enables QUIC 0-RTT on the transports covered by `SEC-022` MAY be exposed at this stage.

## 4. TLS session resumption policy

### 4.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-008.** Heimdall MUST use stateless TLS 1.3 session tickets as the sole session resumption mechanism on every TLS-protected transport. This applies to DoT on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/2.
- **SEC-009.** Session state carried by a TLS 1.3 session ticket MUST be encrypted with a server-held Ticket-Encryption-Key (TEK), and the ticket MUST be opaque to the client.
- **SEC-010.** Heimdall MUST NOT maintain any stateful server-side session cache for TLS 1.3 session resumption on the transports covered by `SEC-008`.
- **SEC-011.** TEKs MUST be rotated periodically, with older TEKs retained for decryption-only use during a bounded acceptance window after retirement, and each issued ticket MUST be bound to a maximum lifetime. The specific rotation frequency, the duration of the acceptance window for retired TEKs, and the maximum ticket lifetime are operational defaults and are tracked as an open question in section 9. Multi-use tickets are permitted.

The session resumption policy for DNS-over-QUIC and for DNS-over-HTTPS over HTTP/3 is not decided by this section and is tracked as an open question in section 9.

## 5. TLS client-certificate authentication (mTLS) policy

### 5.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-012.** Heimdall MUST support optional TLS client-certificate authentication (mTLS) on TLS-protected transports, configurable per listener. This applies to DoT on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/2.
- **SEC-013.** mTLS MUST be disabled by default on every listener. mTLS MUST NOT be active on a listener unless it has been explicitly enabled in that listener's configuration.
- **SEC-014.** When mTLS is enabled on a listener, the server MUST request a client certificate during the TLS handshake and MUST validate the presented certificate against the configured trust policy. A handshake that fails client-certificate validation MUST be rejected and MUST NOT expose the DNS or HTTP input path.
- **SEC-015.** Code paths implementing client-certificate validation MUST NOT be reachable when mTLS is disabled on every listener of the running instance. This is a structural gating requirement consistent with `ROLE-003` through `ROLE-007` in [`001-server-roles.md`](001-server-roles.md) and with `NET-009` in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md).
- **SEC-016.** The mTLS policy defined in `SEC-012` through `SEC-015` applies exclusively to DoT and to DoH over HTTP/2. mTLS on TLS-inside-QUIC transports (DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3) is governed by `SEC-031` through `SEC-035` in section 7A.

The mTLS validation policy details (trust store scope, chain validation rules, optional certificate pinning, revocation checking, certificate-to-identity mapping for use in ACLs or rate limiting) are a single cross-transport policy that applies uniformly to TLS-over-TCP mTLS (DoT, DoH over HTTP/2) and to TLS-inside-QUIC mTLS (DoQ, DoH over HTTP/3). They are not decided by this section and are tracked as an open question in section 9.

## 6. QUIC version policy (DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3)

### 6.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-017.** Heimdall MUST support QUIC version 1 as specified by [RFC 9000](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9000) on every QUIC-based transport. This applies to DoQ on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/3.
- **SEC-018.** Heimdall MUST support QUIC version 2 as specified by [RFC 9369](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9369) on every QUIC-based transport. This applies to DoQ on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/3.
- **SEC-019.** Heimdall MUST NOT support QUIC draft versions, Google QUIC (gQUIC), or any other non-standard QUIC variant on any transport.
- **SEC-020.** QUIC version negotiation MUST be performed in accordance with [RFC 8999](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8999) (Version-Independent Properties of QUIC).
- **SEC-021.** Compatible version negotiation as specified by [RFC 9368](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9368) is out of scope at this stage and MUST NOT be implemented. This decision MAY be re-opened when a third standardised QUIC version exists.

## 7. QUIC amplification mitigation and address validation (DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3)

### 7.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-025.** Heimdall MUST enforce the amplification limit defined in [RFC 9000, section 8.1](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9000#section-8.1) on every connection established over a QUIC-based transport. This applies to DoQ on port 853 and to DoH over HTTP/3.
- **SEC-026.** For any new client flow on a QUIC-based transport that does not present a valid, server-issued `NEW_TOKEN`, Heimdall MUST perform address validation by sending a Retry packet in accordance with [RFC 9000, section 8.1.2](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9000#section-8.1.2), and MUST NOT exchange substantive application data on that flow until the Retry token has been echoed back by the client.
- **SEC-027.** Heimdall MAY issue `NEW_TOKEN` tokens in accordance with [RFC 9000, section 8.1.3](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9000#section-8.1.3) for use in subsequent connections. A deployment MAY be configured so that Heimdall never emits `NEW_TOKEN`; in that configuration every new connection incurs a Retry under `SEC-026`.
- **SEC-028.** When `NEW_TOKEN` emission is enabled, every `NEW_TOKEN` token issued by Heimdall MUST be single-use. Heimdall MUST reject any `NEW_TOKEN` token that is presented a second time. No configuration option that permits multi-use `NEW_TOKEN` MAY be exposed.
- **SEC-029.** Heimdall MUST enforce the single-use property required by `SEC-028` through a strike-register, or through an equivalent anti-replay mechanism that provides the same guarantee against replay of a previously accepted `NEW_TOKEN` token.
- **SEC-030.** The key used to produce `NEW_TOKEN` tokens MUST be rotatable. The concrete rotation frequency, the key retention window for validating tokens issued under retired keys, and the persistence strategy for the strike-register required by `SEC-029` are operational defaults and are tracked as an open question in section 9.

## 7A. QUIC-layer client-certificate authentication (mTLS) policy (DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3)

### 7A.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-031.** Heimdall MUST support optional TLS client-certificate authentication (mTLS) at the TLS-1.3-inside-QUIC layer on QUIC-based transports, configurable per listener. This applies to DoQ on port 853 ([RFC 9250](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9250)) and to DoH over HTTP/3 ([RFC 9114](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9114)).
- **SEC-032.** mTLS MUST be disabled by default on every QUIC listener. mTLS MUST NOT be active on a QUIC listener unless it has been explicitly enabled in that listener's configuration.
- **SEC-033.** When mTLS is enabled on a QUIC listener, the server MUST request a client certificate during the QUIC handshake and MUST validate the presented certificate against the configured trust policy. A handshake that fails client-certificate validation MUST be rejected and MUST NOT expose the DNS or HTTP input path.
- **SEC-034.** Code paths implementing client-certificate validation on QUIC listeners MUST NOT be reachable when mTLS is disabled on every QUIC listener of the running instance. This is a structural gating requirement consistent with `ROLE-003` through `ROLE-007` in [`001-server-roles.md`](001-server-roles.md), with `NET-009` in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md), and parallel to the gating established by `SEC-015` for TLS-over-TCP mTLS.
- **SEC-035.** The mTLS policy defined in `SEC-031` through `SEC-034` applies exclusively to the TLS-1.3-inside-QUIC layer on DoQ and on DoH over HTTP/3. The validation policy applied to client certificates under `SEC-033` is a single cross-transport policy shared with the TLS-over-TCP mTLS defined in section 5, as stated in section 5.1 and tracked as an open question in section 10.

## 8. HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 hardening (DoH over HTTP/2 and DoH over HTTP/3)

### 8.1 Normative requirements

- **SEC-036.** Heimdall MUST apply the hardening mitigations required by `SEC-037` through `SEC-045` explicitly at the Heimdall server layer for every DoH listener. Heimdall MUST NOT rely solely on the default behaviour of the underlying HTTP/2 or HTTP/3 libraries to enforce any of those mitigations. This applies to DoH over HTTP/2 ([RFC 9113](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9113)) and to DoH over HTTP/3 ([RFC 9114](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9114)).
- **SEC-037.** Heimdall MUST enforce, on every DoH connection, a total header-block size limit that bounds the aggregate size of a single header block. On DoH over HTTP/2, the limit MUST cover the sum of the initial HEADERS frame and every subsequent CONTINUATION frame that belongs to the same header block. On DoH over HTTP/3, the limit MUST cover the HEADERS frame. A header block that exceeds the limit MUST cause the connection or the stream, as applicable, to be terminated without the header block being processed as an HTTP request.
- **SEC-038.** Heimdall MUST enforce, on every DoH connection, a per-connection cap on the number of concurrent streams. On DoH over HTTP/2, the cap MUST be advertised through the `SETTINGS_MAX_CONCURRENT_STREAMS` setting as defined by [RFC 9113](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9113) and MUST be enforced by the server regardless of any value a peer may announce for itself. On DoH over HTTP/3, an equivalent per-connection stream-concurrency cap MUST be enforced, consistent with [RFC 9114](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9114).
- **SEC-039.** Heimdall MUST enforce an upper bound on the HPACK dynamic-table size used by the decoder on every DoH over HTTP/2 connection. The server MUST NOT accept a dynamic-table size greater than the configured upper bound, regardless of any value signalled by the peer.
- **SEC-040.** Heimdall MUST enforce an upper bound on the QPACK dynamic-table size used by the decoder on every DoH over HTTP/3 connection. The server MUST NOT accept a dynamic-table size greater than the configured upper bound, regardless of any value signalled by the peer.
- **SEC-041.** Heimdall MUST implement rapid-reset detection on every DoH connection, as a mitigation of the CVE-2023-44487 class of attacks. The server MUST maintain a sliding-window measurement of stream-reset events per connection and MUST, when the configured threshold is exceeded, automatically disconnect the offending connection, rate-limit it, or both. This applies to DoH over HTTP/2 and to DoH over HTTP/3.
- **SEC-042.** Heimdall MUST enforce, on every DoH over HTTP/2 connection, a per-header-block cap on the number of CONTINUATION frames that belong to the same header block, as a mitigation of the CVE-2024-27983 class of attacks. A header block that exceeds the cap MUST cause the connection to be terminated without the header block being processed as an HTTP request.
- **SEC-043.** Heimdall MUST enforce, on every DoH connection, rate limits on control-frame traffic received from the peer. The rate limits MUST cover SETTINGS, PING, RST_STREAM, and PRIORITY frames on DoH over HTTP/2, and MUST cover the equivalent control traffic applicable under [RFC 9114](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9114) on DoH over HTTP/3. A connection that exceeds any of these rate limits MUST be terminated or rate-limited, at the server's discretion, without further application data being processed.
- **SEC-044.** Heimdall MUST enforce a timeout on the completion of every header block on every DoH connection. A header block whose reception is not completed within the configured timeout MUST cause the connection or the stream, as applicable, to be terminated without the header block being processed as an HTTP request. This applies to DoH over HTTP/2 and to DoH over HTTP/3.
- **SEC-045.** Heimdall MUST enforce bounds on flow-control window sizes on every DoH connection. The initial and the maximum window sizes MUST lie within configured ranges, and the server MUST reject or clamp any peer-announced or peer-requested value that falls outside those ranges. This applies to DoH over HTTP/2 and to DoH over HTTP/3, per the flow-control mechanisms defined for each protocol version.
- **SEC-046.** The concrete threshold values, size limits, rate-limit parameters, window sizes, and timeouts that calibrate the mitigations required by `SEC-037` through `SEC-045` are operational defaults. They MUST be set to conservative defaults and MAY be relaxed via configuration for specific deployment profiles. The numeric defaults are tracked as an open question in section 10 and MUST NOT be assumed before they are specified.

## 9. Rationale

TLS 1.3 structurally eliminates several classes of vulnerabilities that are reachable in TLS 1.2 and earlier: RSA key transport, CBC-MAC cipher suites, TLS-level compression, renegotiation, and the downgrade mechanisms associated with multi-version negotiation. The cipher suite set is restricted to AEAD constructions, and the handshake state machine is substantially simpler. These properties align directly with the non-negotiable security posture stated in [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md) and reduce the surface on which parsing or state-machine defects could arise.

Disabling the TLS 1.2 code path in the underlying TLS stack is a surface-reduction measure. It removes the code responsible for legacy cryptographic state handling from the reachable binary, rather than guarding it behind a runtime flag. This is the same structural approach already applied to role activation in [`001-server-roles.md`](001-server-roles.md) and to transport listener instantiation in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md).

TLS 1.3 completes its handshake in one round trip, whereas TLS 1.2 without session resumption requires up to two. Under the performance objectives stated in [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md), where latency and syscall minimisation are explicit design drivers, restricting the server to TLS 1.3 removes a slower path without adding a faster one.

Compatibility is not a barrier in 2026. The stubs, proxies, and recursive resolvers that initiate encrypted DNS connections to public services, including systemd-resolved, getdns and stubby, dnscrypt-proxy, dnsdist, and Unbound, have supported TLS 1.3 for multiple release cycles, and the major public encrypted-DNS operators treat TLS 1.3 as the baseline for their DoT and DoH endpoints. Accepting TLS 1.2 would therefore enlarge the attack surface without enlarging the population of clients that can reach the service.

When the security and performance principles of [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md) conflict, security prevails. Here they agree: TLS 1.3 is both the safer and the faster choice.

Refusing TLS 1.3 early data on DoT and on DoH over HTTP/2 follows the same reasoning. The benefit of 0-RTT is marginal on these transports: both DoT and DoH over HTTP/2 amortise the cost of the handshake over many queries on a single long-lived connection, so 0-RTT only saves latency on the very first query of a new connection. Effective anti-replay protection for 0-RTT requires either a strike-register shared across instances, which turns into a distributed-consistency problem, or per-instance ticket keys, which fragment session resumption. Both alternatives reintroduce significant operational complexity on the security-sensitive path. The amplification risk is also non-trivial: a small amount of early-data can trigger a large DNS response, and DNSSEC-signed answers routinely reach several kilobytes. The industry default reinforces this choice: rustls disables server-side 0-RTT by default. The decision is reversible; enabling 0-RTT later remains a non-breaking change once the telemetry and mitigation infrastructure required to operate it safely are in place.

Selecting stateless TLS 1.3 session tickets as the sole resumption mechanism on DoT and on DoH over HTTP/2 follows directly from the deployment model and from the same security and performance constraints. Only the Ticket-Encryption-Key has to be shared across load-balanced instances, so horizontal scale-out does not require replicating per-session state across nodes; a stateful server-side session cache would turn resumption into a distributed-consistency problem with its own failure modes. Periodic TEK rotation with a bounded acceptance window for retired keys narrows the window during which a compromised TEK remains useful: tickets issued under a TEK that has fallen out of the acceptance window become unrecoverable, which is an intentional operational forward-secrecy property rather than a side effect. On the performance axis, resumption eliminates the certificate signature and the asymmetric key exchange on subsequent handshakes, removing the dominant cost from the hot path. Finally, stateless tickets are the default and best-tested path in the TLS library Heimdall targets, which keeps the implementation aligned with the most exercised code in the dependency.

Making TLS client-certificate authentication optional and configurable per listener is the only model that fits the deployment scenarios Heimdall is expected to cover. A public resolver accepts arbitrary clients and cannot demand certificates; an enterprise or corporate deployment binds access to an internal PKI; a zero-trust inter-service deployment requires mutual authentication on every connection. These scenarios are legitimate and mutually exclusive in their mTLS expectations, so a single global policy would force one of them to operate outside its security model. Defaulting mTLS to OFF minimises attack surface by omission: mTLS is always an explicit operator decision and cannot be enabled by accident through template inheritance or partial configuration. Gating the client-certificate validation code path structurally, so that it is not reachable when no listener enables mTLS, applies the same surface-reduction approach already adopted for role activation in [`001-server-roles.md`](001-server-roles.md) and for transport listener instantiation in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md): disabled functionality is absent from the reachable binary rather than guarded by a runtime flag.

Supporting QUIC version 1 on DoQ and on DoH over HTTP/3 is a compliance and interoperability requirement: without QUIC v1 there is no conformance with [RFC 9250](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9250) and no ability to serve the installed base of QUIC clients. Adding QUIC version 2 on top of v1 extends a small and well-bounded code surface — an additional endpoint configuration choice and the version negotiation packet handler — in exchange for a material improvement in resistance to middlebox ossification, which is the explicit motivation of [RFC 9369](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9369). The QUIC libraries targeted by the implementation (`quinn`, `s2n-quic`) provide native support for both versions, so the incremental implementation cost is modest. Excluding QUIC draft versions, Google QUIC (gQUIC), and other non-standard variants follows the same surface-reduction rationale already applied to TLS 1.2: code paths for non-standard or superseded variants are absent from the reachable binary rather than guarded by runtime flags. Supporting compatible version negotiation under [RFC 9368](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9368) for yet-to-be-standardised future versions is deferred because the test matrix and the negotiation semantics cannot be validated against a version that does not yet exist; the decision is reversible and is set to be re-opened when a third standardised QUIC version is available.

Refusing QUIC 0-RTT on DoQ and on DoH over HTTP/3 extends to the QUIC-based transports the same reasoning already applied in section 3.1 to TLS 1.3 early data on DoT and on DoH over HTTP/2. The latency benefit is marginal on the DNS workload: DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3 carry many queries on a single long-lived connection, so the handshake cost is amortised over the lifetime of the connection and 0-RTT only saves a round trip on the very first query. Effective anti-replay protection for 0-RTT requires either a strike-register shared across instances, which turns into a distributed-consistency problem, or per-instance ticket keys, which fragment session resumption — both alternatives reintroduce significant operational complexity on the security-sensitive path. The amplification concern is stricter over QUIC than over TCP-based TLS: QUIC runs on UDP with source-IP spoofability, and 0-RTT combined with a pre-validated `NEW_TOKEN` can bypass the amplification limit specified by [RFC 9000](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9000), turning the server into a reflection vector. A DNS server is a classic reflection target because responses are routinely larger than queries, and DNSSEC-signed answers amplify the imbalance further. QUIC 0-RTT also lacks forward secrecy for the early-data octets. Industry libraries targeted by the implementation (`quinn`, `s2n-quic`) allow rejecting 0-RTT, and rejection is the defensive alignment. The decision is reversible; activation remains a non-breaking change once the telemetry and mitigation infrastructure required to operate 0-RTT safely are in place.

The amplification-mitigation and address-validation policy in section 7 applies the same reflection-resistance reasoning to the full lifetime of every QUIC flow, not only to the 0-RTT path. A DNS server is a classic reflection target: responses are typically larger than queries, DNSSEC-signed answers reach several kilobytes, and QUIC runs over UDP with IP-spoofability. Enforcing the [RFC 9000, section 8.1](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9000#section-8.1) amplification limit on every connection (`SEC-025`) and requiring a Retry packet on every new flow that does not present a valid, server-issued `NEW_TOKEN` (`SEC-026`) is a structural mitigation, not optional hardening: it makes spoofed-source-address amplification unavailable by construction rather than detecting it after the fact. The cost is amortised for legitimate clients under connection reuse: the first connection pays Retry, and subsequent reconnections within the `NEW_TOKEN` key lifetime skip Retry without weakening the guarantee. Requiring single-use `NEW_TOKEN` (`SEC-028`) enforced through a strike-register or equivalent anti-replay mechanism (`SEC-029`) is the decisive property: a multi-use `NEW_TOKEN` would defeat the mitigation entirely, because a single captured token would allow spoofed-IP amplification against that server until the token expired, whereas a single-use token preserves the performance benefit for legitimate clients without opening that vector. Making the `NEW_TOKEN` key rotatable (`SEC-030`) bounds the lifetime during which a compromise of that key remains useful, on the same forward-secrecy rationale that governs TEK rotation in section 4. Permitting an operator to disable `NEW_TOKEN` emission entirely (`SEC-027`) is a deliberate escape hatch for high-threat deployments: at the cost of a Retry on every connection, it removes the token surface altogether and leaves no reusable address-validation credential in circulation. Adaptive or threshold-based Retry — where Retry is triggered only under suspected attack — was rejected because the triggering heuristic is hard to specify formally, difficult to calibrate across realistic traffic profiles, and vulnerable to low-rate distributed attacks that stay below the threshold on each source while still saturating aggregate output; unconditional Retry on unvalidated flows avoids those failure modes at a cost that is a single round trip on a cold connection.

Extending optional, per-listener, default-OFF mTLS to the QUIC-based transports in section 7A applies to DoQ and to DoH over HTTP/3 the same deployment-coverage argument already made for DoT and DoH over HTTP/2 in section 5. The three deployment scenarios that shaped the TLS-over-TCP decision — public resolver (mTLS out of context), enterprise or corporate access bound to an internal PKI (mTLS frequently required), zero-trust inter-service (mTLS as a hard requirement) — occur on QUIC-based transports with equal legitimacy, and a single global policy would again force one of them outside its security model. Parity of semantics across all encrypted transports is itself a security and operational property: identical default, identical enablement model, identical structural gating, and identical validation policy reduce the configuration surface, simplify the mental model for operators, and eliminate a class of cross-transport misconfigurations in which a client authenticated on one transport would not be authenticated the same way on another. Defining the validation policy once, across both TLS-over-TCP and TLS-inside-QUIC, avoids parallel divergent policies whose drift would be a latent source of incidents. Implementation-wise, the QUIC library targeted by Heimdall (`quinn`) delegates authentication to `rustls`, so the cryptographic and certificate-handling primitives invoked for TLS-inside-QUIC mTLS are the same as those already required by the TLS-over-TCP mTLS decision in section 5; no additional trust-path code is introduced. Gating the QUIC client-certificate validation code path structurally, so that it is not reachable when no QUIC listener enables mTLS, applies the same surface-reduction approach already adopted for role activation in [`001-server-roles.md`](001-server-roles.md), for transport listener instantiation in [`002-transports.md`](002-transports.md), and for TLS-over-TCP mTLS in section 5: disabled functionality is absent from the reachable binary rather than guarded by a runtime flag.

The strict HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 hardening posture required by section 8 for DoH over HTTP/2 and DoH over HTTP/3 rests on the fact that public DoH endpoints share the exposure profile of general-purpose HTTP servers, not the narrower exposure of DNS-specific protocols. The attack classes addressed by `SEC-037` through `SEC-045` — HPACK and QPACK decompression bombs, rapid-reset (CVE-2023-44487), CONTINUATION flooding (CVE-2024-27983), control-frame floods on SETTINGS, PING, RST_STREAM and PRIORITY, incomplete-header-block stalls, and flow-control window abuse — have been actively exploited against high-profile production HTTP stacks, including Cloudflare, Google, Apache, and nginx. [RFC 9113](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9113) and [RFC 9114](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9114) describe these attack classes but do not mandate concrete limits; implementations that rely on the RFC-minimum baseline have been demonstrated to be vulnerable to attacks identified after publication of those RFCs. The hardening posture therefore commits Heimdall at the specification level to apply each mitigation explicitly, per connection, rather than inheriting whatever default the underlying HTTP library happens to ship. Requiring the server layer to apply the mitigations (`SEC-036`) reflects the track record of the HTTP library ecosystem: key mitigations such as rapid-reset detection were added to major HTTP/2 stacks only after the corresponding CVEs, the default calibrations have historically drifted between library releases, and effective protection requires the server to pin its own limits rather than trusting unspecified defaults. The numeric calibration of those limits requires measurement under realistic DoH traffic — header-block sizes, stream-concurrency patterns, control-frame cadence, flow-control dynamics — and therefore is deferred to a subsequent operational-defaults clarification under `SEC-046`; the policy commitment, however, is not deferred. When the security and performance principles of [`../CLAUDE.md`](../CLAUDE.md) conflict for these limits, security prevails: conservative defaults that slightly constrain peak throughput on pathological-looking peers are preferred to default values that preserve peak throughput at the cost of reachable amplification or exhaustion vectors.

## 10. Open questions

The following items are **not yet decided** and MUST NOT be assumed. They are listed here because they are directly downstream of the decisions in this file and will be specified incrementally in subsequent revisions of this document.

- **TLS session-ticket operational defaults.** The TEK rotation frequency, the acceptance window during which retired TEKs remain accepted for decryption only, and the maximum ticket lifetime on DoT and DoH over HTTP/2 are **to be specified**.
- **mTLS validation policy (cross-transport).** The mTLS validation policy is a single cross-transport policy and applies uniformly to TLS-over-TCP mTLS (DoT and DoH over HTTP/2, as fixed by `SEC-012` and `SEC-016`) and to TLS-inside-QUIC mTLS (DoQ and DoH over HTTP/3, as fixed by `SEC-031` and `SEC-035`). The policy items — trust store scope, chain validation rules, pinning, revocation checking (OCSP stapling, CRLs), certificate-to-identity mapping — are **to be specified**.
- **QUIC `NEW_TOKEN` operational defaults.** The `NEW_TOKEN` token-encryption-key rotation frequency, the key retention window for validating tokens issued under retired keys, and the persistence strategy for the strike-register required by `SEC-029` are **to be specified**.
- **HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 hardening numeric defaults.** The concrete threshold values, size limits, rate-limit parameters, window sizes, and timeouts for the mitigations mandated by `SEC-036` through `SEC-045` are **to be specified**.

No implementation activity may proceed on the basis of assumptions about any of the items above.
